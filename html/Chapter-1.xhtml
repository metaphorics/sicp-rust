1 Building Abstractions with Procedures ¶

The acts of the mind, wherein it exerts its power over simple ideas, are chiefly these three: 1. Combining several simple ideas into one compound one, and thus all complex ideas are made. 2. The second is bringing two ideas, whether simple or complex, together, and setting them by one another so as to take a view of them at once, without uniting them into one, by which it gets all its ideas of relations. 3. The third is separating them from all other ideas that accompany them in their real existence: this is called abstraction, and thus all its general ideas are made.

—John Locke, An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1690)

We are about to study the idea of a computational process. Computational processes are abstract beings that inhabit computers. As they evolve, processes manipulate other abstract things called data. The evolution of a process is directed by a pattern of rules called a program. People create programs to direct processes. In effect, we conjure the spirits of the computer with our spells.

A computational process is indeed much like a sorcerer’s idea of a spirit. It cannot be seen or touched. It is not composed of matter at all. However, it is very real. It can perform intellectual work. It can answer questions. It can affect the world by disbursing money at a bank or by controlling a robot arm in a factory. The programs we use to conjure processes are like a sorcerer’s spells. They are carefully composed from symbolic expressions in arcane and esoteric programming languages that prescribe the tasks we want our processes to perform.

A computational process, in a correctly working computer, executes programs precisely and accurately. Thus, like the sorcerer’s apprentice, novice programmers must learn to understand and to anticipate the consequences of their conjuring. Even small errors (usually called bugs or glitches) in programs can have complex and unanticipated consequences.

Fortunately, learning to program is considerably less dangerous than learning sorcery, because the spirits we deal with are conveniently contained in a secure way. Real-world programming, however, requires care, expertise, and wisdom. A small bug in a computer-aided design program, for example, can lead to the catastrophic collapse of an airplane or a dam or the self-destruction of an industrial robot.

Master software engineers have the ability to organize programs so that they can be reasonably sure that the resulting processes will perform the tasks intended. They can visualize the behavior of their systems in advance. They know how to structure programs so that unanticipated problems do not lead to catastrophic consequences, and when problems do arise, they can debug their programs. Well-designed computational systems, like well-designed automobiles or nuclear reactors, are designed in a modular manner, so that the parts can be constructed, replaced, and debugged separately.

Programming in Rust ¶

We need an appropriate language for describing processes, and we will use for this purpose the programming language Rust. Just as our everyday thoughts are usually expressed in our natural language (such as English, French, or Japanese), and descriptions of quantitative phenomena are expressed with mathematical notations, our procedural thoughts will be expressed in Rust.

Rust is a modern systems programming language focused on safety, speed, and concurrency. While the original edition of this book used Lisp (specifically Scheme) to demonstrate these concepts, we will explore them through the lens of Rust. This offers a modern perspective on abstraction: where Lisp is dynamically typed and garbage collected, Rust is statically typed and uses a unique ownership model to manage memory without a garbage collector. Despite these differences, the core principles of abstraction and modularity remain universal.

Rust was born at Mozilla Research, with a vision to create a language that was both safe and fast. It reached version 1.0 in 2015 and has since gained wide adoption for its ability to prevent entire classes of bugs (like null pointer dereferencing and data races) at compile time.

Why are we using Rust as the framework for our discussion of programming? Because it forces us to think clearly about the ownership and lifetime of our data, which are crucial concepts in modern software engineering. While Lisp blurs the distinction between data and code through its uniform structure, Rust achieves powerful abstractions through its trait system and zero-cost abstractions. This allows us to write high-level code that compiles down to efficient machine instructions.

Using Rust for these exercises will challenge you to think about types and memory in a way that dynamic languages do not, providing a rigorous foundation for understanding how computer systems actually work.
